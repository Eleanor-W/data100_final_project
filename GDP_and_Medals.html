GDP Per Capita

An Overview of GDP per Capita

What is GDP per Capita?

Gross Domestic Product per Capita, referred to simply GDP per Capita, is a measure of economic output. GDP per capita provides a rough estimate of total amount of goods and services per person in a given country. The World Bank calculates GDP per capita by dividing Real GDP by mid-year population size. Real GDP is the total of all goods and services produced by the country, then adjusted for inflation.

Why does it matter?

Aside from obvious economic usages, GDP per Capita is often used to evaluate the standard of living in a country. In the past, the United Nations considered GDP per capita when determing Human Development Index(HDI) values for the countries of the world. Since 2010, however, the UN has used different economic measures, but GDP per capita still remains an accurate and prevalent measure of development. A high GDP per capita typically indicates a high level of standard of living in a country, with many of the countries with the highest GDP per capitas also having some of the highest rankings on the HDI.

Looking at the graph, we see that countries with higher GDP per capita typically performed better in the games. The top three countries when it comes to medals are Norway with 26 medals, the United States with 28 medals, and Russia with 29 medals. The US and Norway follow the general pattern we see with GDP per capita and number of medals. In 2014, Norway's GDP per capita was $97,666, placing as the country with the 5th highest GDP per capita in the entire world at the time. On a similar note, the USA also placed in the top 20 for GDP per capita at 17th with a GDP per capita of $55,123. The only major exception we see in these games is Russia, the country with the most medals, yet the 5th lowest GDP per capita out of all medalling countries, and ranked 76th out of all the countries in the world, with a GDP per capita of $14,095. It should be noted, however, that this graph only includes countries that won at least one medal, so there could be some other outliers (wealthy countries winning no medals), but for the most part we see a possible positive correlation between GDP per capita and Number of Medals.
